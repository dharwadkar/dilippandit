Eaglenest Wildlife Sanctuary, District Bomdila, Arunachal Pradesh, India

Trip Report from 19-23 May 2009

I spent five wonderful days in Eaglenest Wildlife Sanctuary (Arunachal Pradesh) in the company of Dr. Ramana Athreya and Mr. Sanjay Sondhi, a naturalist from Dehradun.

We spent 2 days at Lama and 2 days in Bompu camps. The Bugun Welfare Society run by Mr Indi Glow, who arranges stays in these camps and he manages them very well. The staff employed to look after the needs of birders can be relied upon to provide excellent boarding and lodging arrangements. The only area of concern is the condition of roads which for most part is good but can be really bad and bumpy at several places along the 50 km route from Tenga to Bompu. I feel that one ought to be thankful for the not-so-good quality of roads because inaccessibility of these camps has actually saved these intact forests and wildlife in them from certain destruction.

A Delhi birder who comes here for the first time would be amazed at the number of lifers he would see here. More extraordinary and wonderful, from the birding point of view, is the routine use of recorded calls and songs of birds by bird guides in flushing birds out of deep forests and thick bushes and undergrowth. I do not think one would be able to see even half as many species of birds without the use of playback facility of calls as one would with it. The widespread use of recorded calls of birds in EWS by the bird guides might, I wonder, with the passage of time, make birds wary of calls and not respond to the playback of calls at all. I am sure there is justification for the use of playback facility of calls as without it; it is difficult to see birds in bamboo thickets and thick undergrowth. One can only expect to hear them. Besides in this terrain it is difficult to go near a bird, restricted as one is by steep hills on one side and the precipitous valleys on the other not to mention thick forest and undergrowth cover.

The weather held out for most of my stay. We reached Eaglenest in the evening when the sky was overcast and rain threatened to play spoilsport. As we drove up the hilly slope we saw a party of birders led by Ramki with his huge lens and monkey cap descend from the opposite direction on foot. Shashank Dalvi, the birder most in demand in Eaglenest, eagerly informed us that he had seen Hodgson’s Hawk Cuckoo and whether we would like to see it. He then played the call of this bird and sure enough after a few minutes of wait the bird was flying overhead hopping from one branch to another. Unfortunately for us the weather was so bad that we did not get good views of this rare bird. Later that evening we heard the calls of Indian, Oriental, Lesser, Large hawk and Plaintive cuckoo. Large hawk’s call is similar to that of Common Hawk’s. The only difference being a trill at the end which can only be discerned if one has heard it often enough. Large Hawk’s call was heard most frequently in Eaglenest around Lama camp.

On the first day we got up at 5 am in the morning thinking that we would catch birds at the first light. But imagine our shock when we found, as we opened our tent flaps, that the Sun was shining as brightly as it would at 7 am in Delhi in June. Although we could hear the calls of birds as we lay on our beds, the darkness inside the tent gave us a false sense of darkness outside it. After that we decided to get up at 4 am and be out by 4.30 am. First lesson in birding in EWS learnt at no great cost. Even my co-birders who were not new to EWS were taken aback.

The sky was azure and the Sun mildly warm. I found that one of my co-birder was wearing a thin hand glove that did not make any sense to me at all. When queried he said that it was worn to protect the hand from attacks by small insects that buzz all around one. These insects bit my hand several times but in the excitement of watching birds, one tends to ignore small pinpricks. But now as I sit back in my home keying in this report, both my hands and feet are sore with small wounds caused by ticks initially and later by my mindless and stupid scratching of the bites.

In the afternoon, Shashank led us to a couple of spots where the newest discovery that made Eaglenest famous among birding community all over the world, Bugun Liocichla, is generally seen. Try hard as he might with the bird’s recorded calls; it did not come out of its self-imposed territorial restriction. Dejected we all started walking back to our camp in darkness. However the disappointment of not being able to see the “star” bird was somewhat mitigated by the fortuitous catch of three snakes in a space of half an hour. Shashank is an expert catcher of all reptiles. Two of these snakes were Trachischium monticola – a harmless variety. They were about 18 inches long. The third snake most probably was a keelback (?). These snakes were handled by the Shashank as if they were pieces of thick cotton ropes. When we reached the Lama camp another bottle was brought in which was kept a monocled cobra about 15” long. It is reported that no anti-venom drug has been produced to counter the effect of its highly venomous and deadly bite. This cobra was again caught by Shashank near Ramlingam village.

We finally managed to see Bugun Liocichla the next day in the morning near the nallah about 4 km from Lama camp towards Ramlingam village. It was a male and a beautiful bird to boot. A little further ahead we saw black-tailed crake near Ramlingam village. It responded beautifully to its call. Without Shashank it would have been difficult to see either bird.

The first night was devoted to an experiment which Ramana Athreya devised. He hung a white cotton sheet about 4 feet X 4 feet size in an open ground and lit a 100 W bulb on the top of it. Within half an hour scores of moths were clinging to the white sheet. Some of these moths were 6 inch large and some tiny. But wing patterns and colours of all these moths were so beautiful that both Ramana and Sondhi kept on taking their photographs well into the night. Their zeal infected me too and I too found myself taking photographs of moths that I found were extraordinarily large or beautiful.

My best birding was in the area around Bompu camp. The place is teeming with birds and most of them great skulkers. Two birds that gave me trouble no end in Itanagar were finally seen here. Rufous capped babbler and grey bellied tesia – thanks to the playback facility. The latter was seen just briefly but it came close before going back in thick cover. The 13 km walk through dense forest from Bompu to Sessni was a most unforgettable experience. Out of this world. The forest cover was so thick on either side of the valley that one kept asking oneself time and again whether it was for real. And how many species of birds, reptiles, amphibians, butterflies could these forests be hiding in their bosom - waiting to be discovered. And to cap the ethereal experience was the languorous floating of misty clouds through which one passed time and again. All this made photography impossible but no one was complaining. My co-birder, Sondhi an IITK alumnus, who gave up an attractive corporate job at a young age of 43 to pursue his passion for wildlife was at a loss to decide whether to look up for birds or to look down for frogs, lizards, butterflies and snakes of which he collected a sizable number of specimen for scientific study. He kept the frogs captured by him in bags and brought them to his tent. Since my tent was next to his, I heard the croaking of frogs all night that was made by his prisoners more in protest at being held under bag arrest without so much as by your leave, than in any other emotion. Or so I believe. When I told him, in a tone that was a strange mixture of request and command, to release the poor creatures, he replied a little indignantly by telling me not to talk to him like his wife (!!) who also apparently pesters him for keeping all manner of wildlife in his house for jotting down their sizes and other taxonomic details and makes life hell for him for not releasing them back in their original habitat which he eventually does as soon as he notes down the details and photographs them. I have yet see a more indefatigable birder and wildlife enthusiast. He would be out of camp at 4 in the morning after partaking of most frugal breakfast and would remain on shank’s mare birding and collecting specimen of butterflies, toads and frogs till evening without any food during the day. He would then have dinner before resuming his work of photographing his catch and noting down taxonomic details. And only thereafter would he hit the sack at 10 pm in the night.

Beyond Sissni lies the Hodgson’s frogmouth zone. And we went there more out of curiosity than in any real expectation of seeing the bird. There was a path or what looked like a path that took one down a steep slope through a bamboo grove to the location where this nocturnal bird is usually seen. But by the time we reached this place we were tired, but not our bird guide, Tenspo Tesring, who was surprised at out refusal to go down the path with him. He thought we were crazy. But I knew that a false and careless step on a loose boulder could take one tumbling down the slope with no hope of medical attention for miles around. So “thanks but no thanks” was our reply to his entreaties. He went alone down the slope, played the call several times to no avail and came out only after we told him that we would very much like to reach the camp before night fall.

The last morning was the most productive of all. It was sunny and there was a pleasant nip in the air. We walked for about 3 hours during which we saw many birds among which was green cochoa. Its coloration is different from the one given in the illustration in Inskipp. Its cheek is white and not green. My guide told me that one Englishman has taken its photo and would get the plate corrected after talking to the authors of the book.

Thereafter I left Eaglenest. I missed out seeing Hodgson’s Frogmouth, Blue-fronted Robin, Ward’s Trogan, Slender-billed scimitar babbler and pygmy wren babbler and many more. When I mentioned the list of birds I could not see to Ramana he said that if you see everything you want to see in the first visit, there would be no motivation to come for a repeat visit. One does not need a list of missed birds to pay a second visit to Eaglenest!! You are hooked for life.
